A Summer Curriculum · 2026

Shreya's AI Adventure

Summer Curriculum · 13 sessions
Built for a curious 10-year-old & her PhD parents Four threads, one exhibition

This is a thirteen-week journey, not a class. It is built to work for a curious ten-year-old and for two PhD parents at the very same time — no baby-talk, no jargon walls. We start at the deep end (a question worth thirteen weeks) and end with Shreya standing in front of a real, published exhibition that she made and explains herself.

The curriculum is organized into four Parts you can scan in thirty seconds: history → mechanics → building → demo day. Inside each Part, every session is a card. Click a card to open its structured outline (for parents and educators), and inside that you'll find full, build-ready session notes written so they can be pasted straight into Claude to generate slides and visuals.

Running through all of it are four cross-cutting threads — distinct from the per-session "journey stage" pills. Here's the difference, so nothing gets confused this time:

The four threads

These weave through the whole summer. A thread is the storyline; a journey-stage pill (on each card) just tells you where in the arc that session sits.
1 · The Story

How AI was built, and by whom — a human story of people, dreams, and accidents stretching back to fire.

2 · The Intuition

What's actually going on inside, built as gut-feeling and pictures — never as math.

3 · The Exhibition

"Human Eye · AI Eye" — Kanchan dada's photographs beside AI images of the same subjects, published live on GitHub Pages for demo day.

4 · Why Bother?

"If AI can do so much, why learn anything?" Planted in Session 1, answered by Shreya herself in Session 13.

Journey-stage pills (e.g. Opening Question, First Build, Showtime) appear on each session card to show the trajectory of the summer. They are not the four threads — they're the path.

Tue, June 23 Ready

The longest story ever told

AI didn't start this year. It started with fire. Today we walk the whole story backwards — from a self-driving Waymo all the way to the first spark — and find a question so big it takes thirteen weeks to answer.

A backwards journey through every generation that asked "what could be made simpler?" — ending on a question Shreya will answer herself on demo day.

Journey stage: Opening Question Threads: Story + Why Bother?
▶
The Story (concept)
AI is machines mimicking things humans do with their minds and senses — seeing, understanding language, deciding, creating. The history of AI is really the history of every tool humans built to make something simpler, walked backwards from today's frontier to the first fire, then forwards again through a surprising lens (the Jevons Paradox).
Hands-on
The Backwards Timeline card game: each family member gets invention cards (Waymo, video call, telephone, recorded music, camera, printing press, writing, fire) and places them walking backwards in time. Then everyone replays them forwards, and at each card asks the Jevons question: "What new things did this let humans do that they couldn't before?"
Show-off moment
Shreya names any one invention and, in under 30 seconds, says the one thing it made simpler and one brand-new thing it opened up. ("The camera made remembering a face simpler — and now there are more artists making pictures than ever.")
Why bother?
Plant the question and do not answer it: "If AI can do olympiad math and beat world champions, why should you bother learning anything? Hold that question. It takes thirteen weeks to answer — and you'll answer it yourself on demo day."
Project thread
First AI Journal entry — "What would I automate, and what should stay human?" Three answers in each column, in her own words.
Artifact
An interactive automation timeline (the card game, captured) that becomes the first page of her exhibition site.
📄 Full session notes (build-ready)

Status: Ready — complete beat-by-beat script. Paste any beat into Claude with "make slides / find visuals for this beat."

Total arc: ~75–90 min with breaks. The spine is backwards in time, then forwards through the Jevons lens. A 10-year-old needs visual anchors, so every beat lists suggested images/videos. Keep the adults engaged with the real dates and the real intellectual tension; keep Shreya engaged with the pictures and the card game.

Beat 1 — "What does AI even mean?"

Open with the question, not an answer. Let everyone guess first. Then land the honest, kid-accessible definition: "AI is when we build machines that mimic things humans normally do with their minds and senses — seeing, understanding language, deciding, and creating." Note for the parents: that's deliberately broad and behavior-based, which is honest — there is no single agreed technical definition, and that's part of the story.

Visual: a split screen — a human eye and a camera lens; a child talking and a phone showing speech-to-text. Short clip of a phone describing a photo aloud.
Beat 2 — Cool things she'd love TODAY

Make it desirable and concrete: (a) self-driving cars — she may have seen Waymos around town; (b) a robot that cleans up toys and books after a study session or a playdate; (c) robots doing chores and heavy work people don't enjoy. Frame these as "wouldn't it be amazing if…" — we'll come back to which ones are real.

Visual: Waymo driverless-car footage (by late 2025 Waymo ran a unified 260+ sq-mi service area and began freeway rides); a home-robot demo reel. Keep it short and wondrous.
Beat 3 — Walk BACKWARDS through the generations

The big reveal: none of this started today. Each generation asked "what could be made simpler?" and built an answer. Walk back, one vivid stop at a time:

  • Video calling — Shreya video-calls family in Kolkata and Seattle. Once pure science fiction: The Jetsons promised exactly this in 1962.
  • The telephone — talking across distance (Alexander Graham Bell, 1876).
  • Recorded music — capturing a sound so you can hear it again forever.
  • The camera — keeping a moment without painting it by hand.
  • The printing press — Gutenberg, ~1440: copying a book without a scribe.
  • Writing itself — storing a thought outside your own head.
  • Fire & the first tools — the very first time humans bent the world to make life simpler.
Visual per stop: Jetsons video-phone clip → vintage telephone-exchange photo → old gramophone → early box camera → a Gutenberg press → ancient clay tablet/cave wall → cave-painting + early fire. One image per card; she'll reuse them in the game.
Beat 4 — Snap back to TODAY's frontier

Now jump to the present and let it feel almost unfair how much AI can do: olympiad-level math (in 2024 Google's system reached silver-medal standard, 28/42, one point shy of gold; in 2025 Gemini Deep Think reached gold-medal standard, 35/42 — officially graded by the math olympiad); beating world champions at chess (Deep Blue beat Kasparov, 1997) and Go (AlphaGo beat Lee Sedol, 2016); writing, coding, and generating images and video. Honesty note for parents: say "gold-medal standard," not "won a gold medal" — the AI wasn't an official contestant. This beat exists to provoke: "If AI can do ALL this… what are humans even for?"

Visual: a "silver 2024 → gold 2025" scoreboard; the famous AlphaGo "move 37" board; a wall of AI-generated images. Let the provocation hang in the air.
Beat 5 — The experience answer

First answer to the provocation: experiences can't be outsourced. Hearing a piece of music that gives you goosebumps. Eating something delicious. Choosing what to wear. The feeling of finally landing a hard Bharatanatyam step, hitting a softball cleanly, or winning a swim race after weeks of trying. AI can describe every one of these — it can never have them. Nobody can take those away from her.

Visual: a warm montage — dance, piano keys, a swim lane, a family meal. Ask Shreya to name a feeling AI could never feel.
Beat 6 — The survival pushback & the Jevons Paradox

The grown-up counter-punch (great for the PhD parents): "Fine, but experiences need survival. How do we keep eating, working, mattering?" Introduce the Jevons Paradox (William Stanley Jevons, The Coal Question, 1865): when better steam engines made coal use more efficient, Britain burned more coal, not less — because cheap power created brand-new uses. Cheaper-and-better doesn't shrink a world; it grows it. Now replay Beat 3's inventions forwards through this lens: writing didn't kill memory; the printing press didn't make scribes pointless — it created mass literacy; the camera didn't kill painting — there are more visual artists than ever; the phone didn't kill conversation. So today's AI won't empty the world of things to do — it opens problems we couldn't touch before.

Visual: a simple "efficiency up ↑ → usage up ↑" sketch of Jevons; then the timeline cards flipped forward with a "+ new jobs / + new art" stamp on each.
Beat 7 — But AI can't do everything (the wide-open frontier)

Ground the hope in real 2026 limits, honestly: a robot still can't reliably tidy a messy playroom, fold a basket of laundry, or help an elderly grandparent out of bed. Real proof: the 2026 Weave "Isaac 0" laundry robot costs ~$8,000 and still needs a human to remotely fix the tricky folds; the 1X "NEO" home robot leans on remote human helpers for tasks it doesn't know. And from the scientists we read: AI doesn't keep learning after it's built the way a kid does, it doesn't know what anything feels like, and it lacks real common sense about the physical world. Frame it as an invitation: "There is SO much still to figure out — and it's wide open for your generation."

Visual: clip of a robot fumbling laundry; side-by-side "AI is great at ___ / humans still own ___" board. Sources: LeCun–Malik–Dupoux (2026) and Surya Ganguli, Daedalus (2026).
Beat 8 — Plant the question, walk away

Close on the hook, unanswered: "So — if AI can do so much, why should you bother learning anything at all? Don't answer yet. Hold it. It takes thirteen weeks. And on demo day, YOU tell US." Write the question on a card and pin it somewhere she'll see it all summer.

Visual: a single bold card — "Why bother learning anything if AI can do it?" — signed and dated by Shreya, photographed for the exhibition's first page.

Wrap → into the project: open the AI Journal and do the "automate vs. keep human" three-and-three. Capture the card game as the interactive automation timeline — page one of the live site.

The dreamers who imagined thinking machines

Some of the people who dreamed up AI were a movie star, a logician whose great-great-grandson now leads AI, and a code-breaker who helped end a war. The field was built by wildly different humans.

Meet the surprising, very human cast who imagined machines that think — long before computers could.

Journey stage: Meet the Builders Thread: Story
▶
The Story (concept)
AI has a human lineage. Ada Lovelace imagined machines could do more than arithmetic; George Boole turned logic into algebra (true/false → 1/0); Alan Turing asked "can machines think?"; Hedy Lamarr, a Hollywood star, co-invented frequency-hopping that helped pave the way for wireless. Why bother?
If a movie star and a logician from the 1800s could shape this, what could you shape? Learning is how you join a story that's still being written.
Project thread
Journal: pick one builder, draw them, and write one sentence on what they made simpler.

From rules to learning

For decades we tried to tell computers every rule. It didn't work for things like "what's a cat?" The big idea: stop writing rules — let the machine learn from examples, like a kid.

The shift that changed everything: from hand-written rules to learning from examples.

Journey stage: The Big Shift Threads: Story + Intuition
▶
The Story (concept)
Early AI was "expert systems" — humans writing thousands of if-then rules. It broke on messy real-world things. The breakthrough was machine learning: show the system many examples and let it find the pattern itself.
Hands-on
"Rules vs examples" game: first try to write rules for "what makes a dog a dog" (it gets absurd fast), then sort a pile of photos into dog/not-dog and notice how examples teach faster than rules.
Show-off moment
Shreya stumps the adults: she gives a 20-second "rule" for recognizing her favorite animal, then finds the edge case that breaks it.
Why bother?
Machines learn from examples — but you choose which examples matter, and you understand why. That judgment is the part worth learning.
Project thread
Journal: collect 5 example photos of one subject Kanchan dada might shoot (for later: Human Eye · AI Eye).

How a machine sees

A photo is just a giant grid of numbers. AI "sees" by spotting patterns in those numbers — edges, then shapes, then "that's a face." No eyes required.

Inside computer vision — how pixels become "I see a cat."

Journey stage: Peek Inside I Threads: Intuition + Exhibition
▶
The Story (concept)
Images are grids of numbers (pixels). Vision models build understanding in layers: first tiny edges, then textures, then parts, then whole objects. It's pattern-stacking, not "seeing."
Hands-on
Graph-paper pixel art: color a tiny grid, then read out the "numbers"; zoom out to see the picture emerge — feel how detail lives in the grid.
Show-off moment
Shreya turns a 5×5 grid of numbers into a recognizable shape in front of the family in under 30 seconds.
Why bother?
AI sees patterns; humans see meaning. Knowing the difference is how you stay the boss of the tool.
Project thread
With Kanchan dada: choose the exhibition's subjects (a flower, a face, a Kolkata street) and shoot the first "human eye" photos.
Artifact

How a machine guesses the next word

A chatbot is basically the world's best "finish my sentence" player. It doesn't know what it's saying — it predicts what word probably comes next, over and over.

The surprisingly simple trick behind chatbots — prediction, not understanding.

Journey stage: Peek Inside II Thread: Intuition
▶
The Story (concept)
Language models predict the next word from everything before it. Trained on enormous text, they get spookily good — but it's prediction, not understanding. That's why they can sound confident and still be wrong.
Hands-on
Human "next-word" game: family builds a silly story one predicted word at a time; then play "autocomplete charades" with phone keyboards.
Show-off moment
Shreya predicts the next word in three of Mom's sentences before Mom finishes — and explains how she "trained" on knowing Mom.
Why bother?
It guesses words; you decide if they're true. Knowing things is how you catch a confident machine being wrong.
Project thread

How a machine makes a picture

Image generators start with pure static — like TV snow — and slowly clean it into a picture, guided by your words. Creativity from noise.

Inside image generation — turning random noise into art with a sentence.

Journey stage: Peek Inside III Threads: Intuition + Exhibition
▶
The Story (concept)
Diffusion models learn to remove noise. Start from random static and, step by step, "denoise" toward an image that matches the words you gave — guided creativity, not copying.
Hands-on
"Static to picture" flipbook: scribble random dots, then erase toward a drawing in stages — physically act out denoising. Then generate the first real "AI eye" images for the exhibition.
Show-off moment
Shreya explains "it starts from TV snow and cleans it up" while flipping her flipbook — under 30 seconds.
Why bother?
AI can make a thousand pictures; you decide which one is good, and why. Taste and judgment are yours.
Project thread

Artifact

What AI still can't do

A robot still can't reliably fold your laundry or tidy your room — and a $8,000 one in 2026 still needs a human helping from afar. The hardest things for AI are the easy things for a 10-year-old.

The honest frontier — where today's AI breaks, and why that's exciting.

Journey stage: The Honest Frontier Threads: Intuition + Why Bother?
▶
The Story (concept)
The real 2026 limits, from the scientists we read: AI doesn't keep learning after it's built; it's weak in the physical, hands-on world; it doesn't know what anything feels like; and it lacks real common sense. The things that are hardest for AI — tidying a room, learning a new dance by feel — are easy for Shreya.
Hands-on

Show-off moment

Why bother?
The unsolved list is huge — and it's hers to work on. The best reason to learn is that the frontier is wide open.
